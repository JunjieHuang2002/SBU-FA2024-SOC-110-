Fire history in British Columbia, Canada

Explore the causes of British Columbia, Canada's frequent wildfires and their impact on neighboring countries.

Introduction

In the last century, fires in British Colombia were infrequent and of low severity, occurring on average once every 10-30 years and were mainly of low- to moderate-severity [1].

However, it has experienced its four most severe wildfire seasons (2017, 2018, 2021, and 2023) of the last half-century during the past 7 years, all of which were marked by weather extremes [2].

Harnessing fire (pre-1912)

The first nations (Indigenous) saw fire as beneficial for biodiversity and cultural continuity. They also viewed fire positively but used it for different objectives, such as land clearing and agriculture.

(Credit: The Enchanted Forest)

Controlling fire (1912-1975)

Timber is an important source of income in British Colombia. In 1912 the government published the Forest Act that controls fires through prevention and suppression.
The Forest Act prohibits "wilfully set fire". These particularly impact Indigenous peoples, who attempt to continue fire prevention efforts but are forced to conform to settler worldviews and goals.

(Credit: University of California)

Emulating fire (1976-1995)

There was a growing recognition that fire played an important ecological role in many of ecosystems. Fire began to be viewed as a natural event integral to ecosystem sustainability, rather than solely as a destructive force.
In this eras, the government reintroduced and mimicked natural fires but still focus on their fire control goals.

(Credit: CNN)

Siloing fire (1995-2017)

Creation of the BC Wildfire Service as a standalone organization separating it from forestry management. This lead to conflicting objectives and uncertainty in addressing catastrophic fire seasons. This disharmony ultimately left them with insufficient resources to deal with the “2003 Firestorm”.

Reasons of Fire - Human Factors

Through the history of fire management of British Columbia, we can analyze some reasons why severity of wildfire became higher:
  • Interruption of Indigenous cultural burns - Indigenous peoples in British Columbia have practiced cultural burning for thousands of years to manage landscapes and ecosystems. Colonial policies and fire suppression strategies interrupted these traditional practices, causing fuels to accumulate and forest resilience to decline.
  • A build-up of forest fuels - Over-emphasis on the commercial value of forests, coupled with decades of fire suppression policies, has led to the accumulation of undergrowth and dense trees in forests. This excess fuel has created conditions for wildfires.
  • Lack of understanding of fire ecology - Early fire management policies considered all fires as destructive fires and focused solely on suppression.
  • Internal conflicts - Conflicting objectives among different agencies and stakeholders complicate fire management.

Other Reasons of Fire

Topography

British Columbia's complex topography, especially the Coast Mountains, creates a rain shadow in the interior. These areas receive less raining, resulting in a dry climate that is more prone to fires.

Climate Change

Overheating is causing Canada's summers to become hotter, windier and more erratic, with some areas experiencing less summer rainfall.
Fire seasons are starting earlier, lasting longer and being harder to control [4].

Lightning

Most wildfires in B.C. are started by lightning strikes. When lightning strikes an object it can release enough heat to ignite a tree or other fuels.

Impacts on Other Countries

2023 Canadian wildfires

Beginning in March 2023, and with increased intensity starting in June, Canada was affected by a record-setting series of wildfires. All 13 provinces and territories were affected, with large fires in Alberta, British Columbia, the Northwest Territories, Nova Scotia, Ontario, and Quebec. The 2023 wildfire season had the most area burned in Canada's recorded history, surpassing the 1989, 1995, and 2014 fire seasons, as well as in recorded North American history, surpassing the 2020 Western US wildfire season [5].

Air pollution in NYC

Smoke from the fires spread widely across Canada and the United States, causing poor air quality conditions in major cities. For example, New York City's air quality levels reached hazardous levels, and the sky turned an eerie orange.

What can we do?

The 2023 Canadian wildfires have shown that wildfires are not just a local issue, but a global one. The impacts of these fires have been felt across the world, with smoke and air pollution spreading to other countries. To prevent future wildfires and their impacts, we need to take action now.
